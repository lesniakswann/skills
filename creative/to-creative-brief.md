# To Creative Brief

Use this skill to turn established context into a clear, structured creative brief.

Use the current conversation and any available supporting materials, including documents, assets, brand guidelines, previous briefs, notes, files, or reference material.

Do not run a briefing interview. Do not ask discovery questions before drafting unless the user has not provided enough context to produce even a partial brief. Synthesize what has already been established and clearly mark what is missing.

## Process

1. Review the current conversation and available reference materials.
2. Identify the project, objectives, audience, deliverables, constraints, context, stakeholders, approvals, timeline, and production implications where available.
3. Distinguish between confirmed information, assumptions, and open questions.
4. Present a concise outline of the sections to include.
5. Ask the user to confirm that the structure reflects their expectations.
6. After confirmation, produce the creative brief.

## Evidence Rules

- Treat explicitly stated information as confirmed.
- Treat reasonable but unconfirmed inferences as assumptions.
- Treat missing decisions, unresolved facts, or information requiring user judgement as open questions.
- Do not invent facts to make the brief feel complete.
- Do not prescribe creative concepts, design routes, campaign ideas, copy lines, or executional recommendations unless they were already established.
- Describe creative considerations without turning them into a creative solution.

## Creative Brief Template

Use the following structure unless the user confirms a different outline.

### Project Summary

Provide a concise overview of the project and why it exists.

### Background

Summarize relevant business, campaign, or organizational context that explains why this work is being commissioned now.

### Objectives

List the prioritized outcomes the work should achieve.

Focus on business and communication objectives rather than creative execution.

### Target Audience

Describe the intended audience, including any relevant demographics, behaviors, motivations, or existing relationship with the organization.

If multiple audiences exist, distinguish between primary and secondary audiences.

### Key Message

State the single most important thing the audience should take away.

Include supporting messages where appropriate.

### Deliverables

List the required assets.

Include known formats, channels, dimensions, languages, quantities, versions, adaptations, or variants where established.

### Creative Considerations

Summarize anything likely to influence the creative approach, including:

- Brand positioning
- Existing campaigns
- Tone of voice
- Visual direction
- Accessibility considerations
- Mandatory content
- Calls to action
- Existing assets to reuse
- Inspiration or references

### Production Considerations

List anything affecting production, including:

- Print specifications
- Digital platforms
- Technical requirements
- Animation or video requirements
- Asset ownership
- Third-party approvals
- Legal or compliance requirements
- Deadlines or launch dates
- Budget considerations, where known

### Constraints

List constraints that the creative team must work within, such as brand guidelines, budget, timeline, mandatory copy, regulatory requirements, existing templates, platform limitations, or client preferences.

### Success Criteria

Describe how success will be judged.

This may include campaign objectives, stakeholder approval, engagement metrics, usability, consistency with brand, or other agreed measures.

### Confirmed Decisions

List decisions that have already been made during discovery.

### Assumptions

List implied but unconfirmed assumptions. Clearly mark these as assumptions.

### Outstanding Questions

List significant unanswered questions that should ideally be resolved before the project is quoted or enters production.

### Out Of Scope

Identify requests, ideas, or deliverables that have been explicitly excluded.

### Further Notes

Add any additional context that may help the creative team understand the project.
